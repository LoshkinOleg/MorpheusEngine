# Sandcrawler Captain

You command a tracked sandcrawler crossing the vast deserts of an unnamed planet. You begin with a small crawler and a crew of roughly 20, somewhere in the open desert with no allegiance and no mission — what you do next is entirely your choice.

## The Planet

An earth-sized world with comparable gravity and a breathable atmosphere, but drastically less surface water. The planet's plate tectonics are nearly stalled, producing a landscape with almost no mountain ranges — instead, immense wind-eroded hardpan flats, sculpted rock formations, and enormous dune seas dominate the surface. The terrain falls somewhere between the Australian bush and the Sahara, with sparse hardy ground cover clinging to rocky outcrops in better areas and total barren waste in others.

The atmosphere is dry. Days are scorching, nights freezing, and temperature swings are severe. Rainfall is exceptionally rare. Sandstorms driven by thermal differentials are frequent and can last for days, forcing crawlers to halt and batten down. Dust devils are constant. Visibility during storms drops to near zero and disrupts radio equipment.

What little surface water exists collects in oases fed by deep ancient aquifers — underground reserves left over from a wetter geological era. Some oases are fed by geothermal upwelling and are more reliable, but most can be over-pumped and exhausted. An oasis running dry is an existential crisis for the city built around it.

Wildlife is limited to small creatures adapted to extreme aridity: burrowing insects, small lizards, beetles that harvest morning condensation. There are no large animals — any that once existed near the water sources were hunted to extinction long ago. Plant life near oases is dense (analogous to date palms and thick scrub), while the open desert supports only sparse, tough ground cover.

## Sandcrawlers

Sandcrawlers are large tracked vehicles that serve as mobile homes, fortresses, workshops, and trading posts. They range from small two-story machines crewed by around 20 people to colossal mobile city-states housing thousands with their own social classes, internal politics, and distinct cultures.

Crawlers operate on 1990s-to-2000s-level technology and are typically heavily armored and armed. Most run on liquid petroleum fuel, the most energy-dense cheap power source available. Some use coal-fired steam engines, though these are heavier, require more crew, and must stay within range of coal-producing regions. A tiny number of the largest crawlers — perhaps only two or three on the entire planet — run on nuclear reactors, a closely guarded technological secret that grants them effectively unlimited range. Nuclear crawlers are near-mythical and the knowledge to build and maintain their reactors is fiercely protected.

Crawlers are accompanied by smaller vehicles carried aboard when not in use: sand bikes, armored cars, boarding trucks, and tanks ranging from WWI to late Cold War vintage. The largest crawlers may even carry a small escort of lesser crawlers.

Armament spans WWI through end-of-Cold-War-era weapons, meticulously maintained by the crew. The heaviest guns are naval-style batteries. Missiles exist but are rare due to their single-use nature and the difficulty of manufacture. All weapons and equipment require constant upkeep — nothing is disposable in this world.

Small crawlers tend toward strict military-style command hierarchies. Larger crawlers may develop complex social structures, specialist guilds, hereditary crew families, and even internal class systems. Some families span multiple crawler crews across generations, never settling in any city.

## Communication and Detection

Radio is the primary means of long-distance communication. There is no orbital infrastructure — no satellites, no global networks. Range is limited by terrain, atmospheric conditions, and equipment quality.

Radio also serves a tactical role. Active radio pulses can be used as a form of sonar to detect distant crawlers, especially in poor visibility, but broadcasting reveals your own position to anyone listening passively. Passive listening preserves concealment but limits detection range. Storms degrade both modes, making ambushes during storm edges a viable tactic. Disciplined crawler fleets may observe radio silence before engagements.

City-states may operate more powerful radio transmitters that can reach neighboring cities, but planet-spanning communication does not exist.

## Navigation

Without satellites, crawlers navigate by dead reckoning, star charts, compass, and odometer. Established routes between major city-states are passed down through tradition and shared knowledge — invisible highways across the sand. Straying from known routes carries real risk: getting lost, running out of fuel or water, or stumbling into hostile territory. The deep desert may hold the remains of crawlers that never arrived at their destination.

## Resources and Economy

The two critical resources are petroleum fuel and water. Fuel lets you move; water lets you live. A crawler rich in one but poor in the other faces very different strategic choices.

Petrol is extracted at dedicated facilities, usually located away from oases. These extraction cities are politically and economically significant enough to import water and other necessities, and they are typically defended by large numbers of mercenary crawlers.

Water comes primarily from oasis city-states. Specialized trading crawlers haul water from oases to industrial cities for profit.

There is no planet-wide currency. Some local coalitions of neighboring city-states may agree on a manufactured token currency, but most trade operates on barter and exchange of services. Common trade goods include scavenged metals, mechanical parts, fuel, water, food, mercenary contracts, and transported cargo.

Mercenaries are abundant — crawlers-for-hire that escort convoys, guard extraction sites, hunt bounties, or simply roam looking for salvage and opportunity.

## City-States

Nearly every oasis supports a city-state of some kind. Their size depends directly on the oasis that sustains them: the smallest subjugated farming settlements may house only a few hundred people, while the largest oasis cities rival medieval London at perhaps two hundred thousand. Cities maintain heavy fixed defenses against roaming crawlers and usually field several large allied crawlers of their own.

Non-oasis cities exist around petrol extraction facilities and occasionally around significant ruins. These depend entirely on imported water and food, making their supply lines a strategic vulnerability.

City-state politics are local. Neighboring cities may form alliances or trade agreements, but the planet is too vast and empty to sustain any large-scale political entity. There is no overarching authority, no international law, no universal code of warfare. Locally, neighbors may observe informal rules — poisoning an oasis might be taboo between neighboring cities — but beyond that, the desert is lawless.

## Society

Humanity on this planet resembles that of the bronze age: fragmented, resourceful, few in numbers, and constantly competing over scarce resources. Societies are generally martial and patriarchal. Slavery is widespread and accepted for captured enemies of a given city-state, though it is not systematic everywhere — emancipation is possible and not dogmatically forbidden in most places.

The relationship between crawler crews and city-dwellers is complex. City-dwellers may view crawler nomads as uncivilized raiders or as essential trading partners, depending on the crawler's reputation. Crawler crews may see city-dwellers as soft but necessary for resupply. Some people live their entire lives aboard crawlers; others never leave their city walls.

## The Old Empire

The planet once belonged to a sprawling interstellar empire. Contact was lost long ago — likely because the planet's scarce resources and low strategic value made it uninteresting to maintain. The empire is now barely remembered. What survives are folk stories, garbled oral traditions, and the physical remains of the empire's presence.

Most ruins have been scavenged thoroughly over the centuries. What remains is mostly structural: foundations half-buried in sand, collapsed installations, the corroded skeletons of infrastructure. A few cities have been built directly around significant ruins — the mangled hull of a crashed starship being mined for metals, for instance.

In extremely remote or hostile regions of the planet, a handful of less-touched ruins may still exist, but they are rare and reaching them is dangerous. Whatever technology they might still contain is not well understood by the planet's current inhabitants.

## Player Starting Conditions

- One small sandcrawler, petroleum-fueled, lightly armed and armored
- Approximately 20 crew members with their own personalities, skills, and relationships
- No fixed allegiance to any city-state or faction
- Starting location is unspecified open desert — the player chooses their own direction
- Basic supplies of fuel, water, food, ammunition — enough to reach the nearest points of civilization if managed carefully
